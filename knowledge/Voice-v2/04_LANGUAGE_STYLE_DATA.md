# 04 — Spoken Language Rules

How to write lines that sound right when *spoken* by the voiceover. The old data here was tuned for reading tweets; spoken promos follow different rules.

**Role in the app:** governs sentence-level writing across every beat of the script.

---

## Sentence Length — SHORT (this inverts the old rule)

Reading tolerates long sentences; the ear doesn't. A voiceover line must fit in **one breath**.

- **Target:** 5–12 words per spoken line.
- **Never:** the old 25–30 word "sweet spot" — that was for reading tweets and is far too long to say.
- **Test:** if you can't say the line in one breath without rushing, cut it.

---

## Person Usage — keep this, it transfers

- **Second person ("you", "your")** — the workhorse. The promo is about the viewer. "You're still chasing that payment."
- **First person ("I", "we")** — for proof and credibility. "We've helped freelancers get paid faster."
- **Best combo:** talk to them, back it with you. "You shouldn't have to chase invoices — so we made them chase themselves."

---

## Cut entirely (reading-only, invisible to the ear)

- **Lowercase styling** — you can't hear case. Write normally; capitalization is for the on-screen text, not the voice.
- **Ellipses for "pacing"** — pacing in voiceover comes from short sentences and pauses, not punctuation.
- **Parenthetical asides** — an aside the ear can't see just clutters the line. Make it its own short sentence or cut it.
- **Emojis, hashtags, "…"-cutoffs** — none of it exists in audio.

---

## Numbers — say them cleanly

- Write numbers as they're spoken: "eleven days," "three hours," "under a minute."
- Never "11d," "3hrs," "<60s" — the model may misread them.
- One number per line max; more than one in a breath gets lost.

---

## Contractions — always

Spoken language uses contractions. "You're," "don't," "we've," "it's." Full forms ("you are," "do not") sound stiff and robotic when read aloud.
