# 02 — Proof & Math Hooks

Hooks that win by showing the numbers. The viewer feels smart for following along and convinced by the time the math lands.

**Best for:** price objections, ROI-driven products, "is this worth it" moments.
**Video beat:** these open on the Hook and carry into Proof (15–22s).
**Hard rule:** every number is the user's real data. Never fabricate metrics — a made-up figure is the fastest way to lose trust.

---

## Hook A — The Real Math

Take a belief the audience holds and disprove it with simple, verifiable arithmetic.

**Neutral example (InvoiceZen):**
> "People think a free invoicing setup saves them money. Let's actually do the math."
> (Spreadsheet: 3 hours a month lost + one late invoice = far more than a paid tool costs.)

**Formula:** `"People think [common belief]" → "let's do the math" → [simple calculation that flips it] → [counterintuitive conclusion]`

**Critical elements:** use real division/multiplication the viewer can follow; the result must contradict the expected answer; keep it simple enough to verify in their head; end on a flat, confident conclusion.

---

## Hook B — The Simple-Change Transformation

A small, almost-too-simple change produced an outsized result.

**Neutral example:**
> "One change got these freelancers paid eleven days faster: automatic reminders."

**Formula:** `[modest starting point] → [notable result] in [short timeframe] / from [one surprisingly simple change]`

**Critical elements:** the result is recent and real; the change sounds trivially simple ("wait, that's it?"); numbers specific, never rounded to look invented.

---

## Hook C — The Provocative Comparison

Put two options side by side so the gap is undeniable. (Good for a social cut of the promo, since it invites reaction.)

**Neutral example:**
> "Spreadsheet invoicing versus this: same work, but one of them chases your late payments for you."

**Formula:** `[option A the viewer uses] vs [option B you offer] → [the one variable that makes B obviously better]`

**Critical elements:** the comparison must be fair and true; one clear differentiating variable; don't manufacture a fake villain — let the real gap speak.

---

## Hook D — The Scale / Efficiency Flex

Lead with a genuinely impressive number, then make its efficiency concrete.

**Neutral example (only if real):**
> "We've helped freelancers recover over two million in late payments — the average user gets paid in days, not weeks."

**Formula:** `[real aggregate metric] / [what it means at the individual level] / [contrast with the slow/expensive alternative]`

**Critical elements:** the scale must be real and yours; translate the big number into something the individual viewer feels; **skip this hook entirely if the user doesn't have real numbers yet.**
